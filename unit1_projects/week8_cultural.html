Pairing June 15, 2014

Pairing was and, honestly, still is the most intimidating feature of the DBC experience for me. With communication emphasized, I find myself occasionally having trouble articulating verbally the coding strategies I attempt to employ. This may sound counterintuitive, but I think the problem occurs from trying to think about a challenge in different ways. I put pressure on myself to commit to the first strategy I think up, whereas I usually tend to sit and mull over a problem when I work it on my own. This pressure is compounded with the main critique I've heard, which is that I don't always fully articulate my thoughts. It is a bad habit of mine not to finish a sentence. I think out loud a lot. For me, it is a problem that is easily caught, and practice hopefully will improve my communication.

So far my pairing sessions have gone well. From the beginning, I have tried to go into them with a positive attitude, with a conscious effort to listen, and with an adequate amount of preparation. I don't know if this is the best strategy, but I think it's a solid one. It pays off in a good pairing session mood, which is essential to have a productive learning session. I've had no new, great insight into pair programming though. I do feel like I haven't used them to their greatest potential. This might be because a good dynamic is difficult to form.

In terms of feedback, I've found that making specific, actionable comments are the most difficult to get down. General problems seem like they're much more prevalent. I'm trying to combat this by being more observant. I've found that sometimes the best critique can be made towards an aspect of a session that wasn't necessarily apparent. What's missing might be the biggest drawback of a bad session.

The biggest problem I have with pairing, though, is initializing it. Reaching out to people is not something I normally do. I have to work on this, and I have to become more comfortable in doing it. It's hard for me to be proactive with people over the Internet. They have lives, schedules, and work I am oblivious to. It is almost like reaching out into the void for me. I'm hoping the different situation of active pairing at DBC will eliminate this personal tension I have. In addition, I believe once I get to know my cohort better after face-to-face meetings, I'll be more comfortable reaching out to them.

Pairing, like many other aspects of learning, is about moving forward and improving. Practice is essential. The more experienced know where the pitfalls are and where the potential lies.
